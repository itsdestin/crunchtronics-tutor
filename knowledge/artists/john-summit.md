# John Summit

## TL;DR
John Summit is named in `../genres/tech-house.md` as Destin's confirmed taste anchor for the genre, and the 5 tracks in this pull confirm it. The Chicago-via-LA accountant-turned-superstar is the cleanest reference point in the library for the contemporary 124-128 BPM tech-house template — punchy kick, rolling sub bass, vocal-chop hook, hypnotic minor-key arrangement.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 128.0-144.9 (mean 139.2). The 128 floor is the tech-house pocket; the 140s outliers are the drum-and-bass-leaning collabs (his "Go Back" with Sub Focus is the obvious example).
- **Common keys:** 12A — C# minor (1 track); 8A — A minor (1 track); 3B — Db major (1 track). Wide spread across the pull. C# minor and A minor are both heavy DJ-set keys.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Kick: layered (sub-808 + transient-shaped click on top), short tail, hits *hard* on every beat.
  - Bass: a single rolling sub note with sidechain-driven pumping and occasional pitch slides — see `../genres/tech-house.md` for the routing.
  - Lead synth: a vocal-formant or "talky" wavetable patch, often a single 2-note motif looped across the drop.
  - Vocal hooks: melancholic, often a featured vocalist (Inéz, Hayla, etc.) doubled an octave up, drowned in long-tail reverb.
  - Drop arrangement is *spacious* — kick + bass + 1 melodic element + percussion; almost everything else drops out for the first 8 bars of the drop.
- **Sample tracks from your library:**
  - LIGHTS GO OUT
  - Where You Are - GRiZ Remix
  - crystallized (feat. Inéz)
  - WITH ME
  - Where You Are - Millero Remix

## Read this yourself
The single most important thing for the curriculum is that `../genres/tech-house.md` already names John Summit as the anchor artist for the entire genre. That means: when Destin starts working on his first tech-house production project in Ableton, the reference track should be a John Summit cut, not a Fisher cut, not a Disco Lines cut. The Beatport "Go Back" breakdown above is the single most useful resource on this page — it's a producer breakdown of an actual John Summit record and covers the kick layering, bass routing, and arrangement decisions step by step.

His career-shape lesson is also Destin-relevant: he was an accountant who taught himself production via YouTube tutorials during 2020 lockdowns. The MusicTech interview captures his philosophy: there are no rules, if it sounds good it sounds good, and the path to good sound is collaborating widely. That's exactly the top-down genre-first approach this curriculum already uses.

For sound-design specifics: when recreating a John Summit kick in Ableton, layer two samples — a sub-heavy 808-style hit with the transient cut, and a separate clicky transient (like a tech-house "tok"). Glue them with a Drum Bus and tune the sub-layer to the project's root key. The `../genres/tech-house.md` cheat sheet covers the rest of the spine.
