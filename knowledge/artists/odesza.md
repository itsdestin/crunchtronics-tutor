# ODESZA

## TL;DR
ODESZA is a Seattle indie-electronic duo (Harrison Mills + Clayton Knight) hand-picked by Destin during the brainstorm. Their signature sound is layered, vocal-forward, anthemic chillwave / future bass — built around chopped vocal samples, lush pads, and tribal-leaning drum programming. Genre-adjacency note: no existing cheat sheet matches cleanly; `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md` is the prereq because of shared emotional register and supersaw-rich pads, but ODESZA sits at ~105-115 BPM with a different drum spine.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 105.0-115.0 (mean 111.7). Tight cluster — this is fundamentally a chillwave / future-bass tempo pocket, not a dance-floor BPM.
- **Common keys:** 3B — Db major (1 track); 11B — A major (1 track); 11A — F# minor (1 track). Major-key lean fits the anthemic-uplift mood.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Vocal samples are the centerpiece — chopped, pitched, layered, often chant-like and processed to feel "tribal" or "wordless." More than half the energy of a typical drop.
  - Drums layer programmed kicks/snares with live-feeling toms, hand percussion, and field-recording-ish textures — not strict EDM kits.
  - Pads are wide and stacked: supersaw layers, formant-shifted choirs, granular textures, all sidechained to the kick.
  - Sub bass sits low and round; the focal melodic energy comes from the chopped vocals + saws, not from a snarling lead synth.
  - Heavy use of vinyl warmth, room reverb, and sidechain pumping for "warm but moving" texture.
- **Sample tracks from your library:**
  - Higher Ground
  - The Last Goodbye
  - Say My Name

## Read this yourself
ODESZA sits in an awkward slot for the curriculum: their production is anthemic and emotionally legible to Destin (which is *why* he picked them), but the exact tempo and drum vocabulary don't match any of the four existing genre cheat sheets. The path forward is to treat them as a future-bass / chillwave reference and study the specific techniques that translate, not the whole catalog as a single project.

What translates cleanly to Ableton Live 12 Lite from day one: chopped vocal samples (Simpler in Slice mode), wide layered pads (Operator can stack saws — though Vital is easier), sidechain pumping (the genre cheat sheets cover the routing), and tribal-leaning drum layering (drag-and-drop one-shots from the stock library). What's harder: the granular textures and the field-recording-blended atmospheres — those want third-party tools.

The MusicRadar interview is the single best entry point on this page. ODESZA's working philosophy — "you can have all the gear in the world, but that doesn't mean you're going to write anything super special" — is the right framing for a beginner. Their actual workflow is one person bringing a loop (chord, drum, sample) to the other and iterating on top of it. That's a translatable creative practice for Destin even before any of the sound-design lessons land.

Honest read on the resource gap: ODESZA never released an official Splice pack, and the third-party preset packs (ODESSA, Desza, etc.) are reverse-engineered tributes by other producers — useful as starting points but not gospel.
