# NGHTMRE

## TL;DR
NGHTMRE (Tyler Marenyi) is an LA-based bass-music producer hand-picked by Destin during the brainstorm. He co-runs the Gud Vibrations label/event series with SLANDER, and his catalog sits across bass-house, hybrid trap, and dubstep — heavier than tech-house, more melodic than pure riddim. The closest existing genre cheat sheet is `../genres/bass-house.md`, though the BPM spread runs wide enough that some tracks are closer to `../genres/dubstep.md` territory.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 145.2-173.7 (mean 157.0). Above the bass-house mid-tempo center and well into dubstep / hybrid-trap territory. The 173 BPM outlier is likely a half-time-felt 87 BPM trap groove.
- **Common keys:** 1A — G# minor (1 track); 4A — F minor (1 track); 4B — Ab major (1 track). The two minor-key tracks fit the heavy-bass default; the major-key track is unusual for the genre and probably a more melodic / vocal-forward cut.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Sound design philosophy is "use organic sounds, not just synthesized ones" — he's said in interviews he prioritizes sounds that don't sound generated, blending field recordings and acoustic source material into electronic patches.
  - Snare layering is a public fingerprint — the 2025 XLNTSOUND DAW-open production breakdown is built around how he stacks multiple snares for the wide, cracking signature hit.
  - Bass design crosses between bass-house growls, hybrid-trap snarls, and dubstep wobbles depending on the track — not a single signature timbre.
  - Drops are punchy and sample-rich, often with vocal chops or dramatic risers carrying the pre-drop tension.
- **Sample tracks from your library:**

  - FEELING GUD
  - GUD VIBRATIONS

## Read this yourself
NGHTMRE is a useful artist on Destin's leaderboard because his work sits *across* multiple existing genre cheat sheets rather than inside one. The 145-174 BPM spread is wider than bass-house's typical 128-140 pocket, which means his catalog touches hybrid trap (the 87 BPM half-time-felt cuts, often "trap" in marketing) and lighter dubstep (the upper-140s tearout-flavored cuts). The right mental model is "Gud Vibrations sound" rather than "bass-house artist" — that's the brand he co-built with SLANDER, and it explicitly blends genres.

The XLNTSOUND production breakdown is the highest-signal resource on this page: NGHTMRE himself opens his Ableton session and walks through the snare-layering technique. That's a rare DAW-native breakdown — most heavy-bass producer interviews stay at the philosophy level. Destin can study the same snare-layering method in Live 12 Lite directly: stack 2-4 snare samples with transient shaping (Lite has Drum Bus + EQ Eight), tune the layers to slightly different frequencies, glue with parallel compression.

NGHTMRE has also said in interviews that he treats Ableton as his core production environment for everything from writing to mastering, and that his sound-design priority is "interesting sounds that haven't been heard before" via organic / field-recorded source material. That last point is a real translatable lesson — it pushes against the temptation to chase preset-only workflows.
