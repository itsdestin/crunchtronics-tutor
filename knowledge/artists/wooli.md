# Wooli

## TL;DR
Wooli (Adam Puleo) is named in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md` as Destin's confirmed master-spec taste anchor for the genre. Only **1 track** appears in the current EDM-playlist pull ("Dopamine," his Tape B collab) — that's under-representation, not lack of taste fit. Expect more Wooli on a future Spotify pull. He's an Ophelia Records mainstay with a former-finance-bro origin story; signature sound is anthemic, supersaw-driven, melodic-dubstep proper.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 144.9-144.9 (mean 144.9). Only 1 enriched row. The 145 BPM aligns with melodic-dubstep's 140 anchor (with modern tempo creep) — confirms genre placement.
- **Common keys:** 6A — G minor (1 track). G minor is one of the four most common melodic-dubstep keys called out in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`'s cheat block; this is genre-default behavior.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Drops are built around supersaw chord stabs carrying the melodic line — the genre-defining technique covered in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`. Less aggressive bass than heavy dubstep, more harmonic content.
  - Vocal hooks are a centerpiece — featured-vocalist topline material processed with formant shifting, pitch correction, and long-tail reverb. "Dopamine" uses an Etta James sample as the core vocal element.
  - Drum pattern: 140 half-time spine, kick on beat 1, snare/clap on beat 3, sparse hats with rolls into drops.
  - Influences he's named publicly: Hans Zimmer (orchestral build moments), Slander, Seven Lions, Dabin (the melodic-dubstep peer group).
- **Sample tracks from your library:**

## Read this yourself
Wooli is load-bearing for the curriculum despite the thin data: `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md` already names him the genre anchor, so when Destin starts his first melodic-dubstep production project the reference track should be a Wooli cut. The 1-track pull is a sampling artifact (the pulled playlists skewed toward bass-house and tech-house). On a future Spotify pull that includes melodic-dubstep playlists, the track count will jump.

The "Dopamine" iEDM interview is the highest-signal resource here: it's a direct producer interview on the actual track in Destin's library, with both Wooli and Tape B walking through the Etta James vocal sample and how the track represents both producers' sounds blended. That's a rare combination of "track in the library" + "interview about that exact track."

The career-shape lesson is also highly Destin-relevant: Wooli taught himself production using Ableton Live, came from a finance job, started in riddim and heavy dubstep, then evolved toward melodic-dubstep as his catalog matured. The interview from "From Finance Bro to Dubstep DJ" (YouTube) reflects this story; the YouTube link is searchable but wasn't directly verified to a current canonical URL during research, so it's not pinned in the references list.

For first production targets: melodic-dubstep is the most accessible genre on Destin's leaderboard for an Ableton Live 12 Lite beginner. Layered supersaws are achievable with stock Operator (or upgraded with Vital, free); the chord-progression-driven structure (`vi-IV-I-V` per `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`) means you can write something that "feels right" before you've mastered sound design. Wooli is the right reference target for that first project.
