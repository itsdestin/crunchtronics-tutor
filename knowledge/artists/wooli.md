# Wooli

## TL;DR
Wooli (Adam Puleo) is named in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md` as Destin's confirmed master-spec taste anchor for the genre. **6 tracks** appear in the current EDM-playlist pull under all-credits counting — solid genre representation, **5 of 6 as collaborator** alongside Subtronics, GRiZ, Excision, ILLENIUM, Seven Lions, and Tape B. He's an Ophelia Records mainstay with a former-finance-bro origin story; signature sound is anthemic, supersaw-driven, melodic-dubstep proper, but his catalog crosses freely into heavy dubstep on the Subtronics and Excision collabs.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 90.0-157.1 (mean ~134 across 4 enriched; 2 unenriched). The 144-145 cluster is core melodic-dubstep ("Dopamine," "Lock In"); 157 is the heavy-dubstep crossover ("Zombie" with Excision and ILLENIUM); 90 is half-time felt 180 ("Don't Look Down" with Excision). Range tells the genre-bridge story: melodic-dubstep core, but pulled into heavier territory by frequent collaborators.
- **Common keys:** 6A — G minor (1), 5A — C minor (1), 9A — E minor (1), 7A — A minor (1); 2 unenriched. All minor — genre default. G minor and C minor are both called out in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`'s cheat block.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Drops are built around supersaw chord stabs carrying the melodic line — the genre-defining technique covered in `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`. Less aggressive bass than heavy dubstep, more harmonic content.
  - Vocal hooks are a centerpiece — featured-vocalist topline material processed with formant shifting, pitch correction, and long-tail reverb. "Dopamine" uses an Etta James sample as the core vocal element.
  - Drum pattern: 140 half-time spine, kick on beat 1, snare/clap on beat 3, sparse hats with rolls into drops.
  - Collaboration adaptation: on Subtronics and Excision collabs he meets them in heavier territory (more aggressive growls, faster tempo); on Tape B and Seven Lions collabs he stays in melodic-dubstep proper. The collab credits are the best window into his range.
  - Influences he's named publicly: Hans Zimmer (orchestral build moments), Slander, Seven Lions, Dabin (the melodic-dubstep peer group).
- **Sample tracks from your library:**
  - Dopamine — with Tape B (the cleanest melodic-dubstep example)
  - Lock In — with Subtronics (heavy-dubstep crossover)
  - Chaos Theory — with GRiZ
  - Zombie (feat. Valerie Broussard) — with ILLENIUM and Excision (cinematic / heavy hybrid)
  - Don't Look Down (Hold On) — with Excision and Codeko (half-time felt)
  - Island — with Seven Lions, Trivecta, and Nevve

## Read this yourself
Wooli is load-bearing for the curriculum: `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md` names him the genre anchor, so when Destin starts his first melodic-dubstep production project the reference track should be a Wooli cut. The 6-track pull (under all-credits counting) confirms strong genre representation — the v1.0 profile's "1 track / under-representation" framing was a single-credit-counting artifact, since Wooli appears predominantly as a collaborator and the primary-only count discarded those credits.

The collab pattern is itself the curriculum-relevant insight: 5 of 6 tracks are co-credits, and the collaborators span the entire heavy-bass leaderboard — Subtronics (heavy dubstep), GRiZ (funk-bass), Excision and ILLENIUM (heavy + cinematic), Seven Lions and Tape B (melodic-dubstep peers). Wooli's genre placement is correctly melodic-dubstep, but his work's actual sonic range is wider because his collaborators pull him in different directions. For Destin, that means the cleanest learning targets are the *non-collab* and *light-collab* cuts: "Dopamine" (Tape B) is the single best example of pure melodic-dubstep in this list because the production is genre-clean.

The "Dopamine" iEDM interview is the highest-signal resource here: it's a direct producer interview on the actual track in Destin's library, with both Wooli and Tape B walking through the Etta James vocal sample and how the track represents both producers' sounds blended. That's a rare combination of "track in the library" + "interview about that exact track."

The career-shape lesson is also highly Destin-relevant: Wooli taught himself production using Ableton Live, came from a finance job, started in riddim and heavy dubstep, then evolved toward melodic-dubstep as his catalog matured. The interview from "From Finance Bro to Dubstep DJ" (YouTube) reflects this story; the YouTube link is searchable but wasn't directly verified to a current canonical URL during research, so it's not pinned in the references list.

For first production targets: melodic-dubstep is the most accessible genre on Destin's leaderboard for an Ableton Live 12 Lite beginner. Layered supersaws are achievable with stock Operator (or upgraded with Vital, free); the chord-progression-driven structure (`vi-IV-I-V` per `../genres/melodic-dubstep.md`) means you can write something that "feels right" before you've mastered sound design. Wooli is the right reference target for that first project — start with "Dopamine" as the model, not the Subtronics/Excision crossover cuts.
