# Levity

## TL;DR
Levity (stylized LEViT∆TE) is a Seattle-area dubstep / tearout producer hand-picked by Destin during the brainstorm. The 4 tracks in the pull skew heavy and tearout-flavored, with one notable mid-tempo outlier ("Postman" at 100 BPM) that points to genre flexibility. Closest genre cheat sheet is `../genres/dubstep.md`; melodic-dubstep is listed as the prereq because Destin's curriculum reaches it before crossing into heavy bass design.

## Cheat block
- **BPM range:** 100.0-144.7 (mean 133.0). The 144.7 cluster is dubstep / tearout proper; the 100 BPM outlier ("Postman") is a half-time-felt mid-tempo cut.
- **Common keys:** 1A — G# minor (1 track); 7B — F major (1 track); 1B — B major (1 track). Spread across the pull — no single key dominant given the small sample.
- **Signature sound design:**
  - Bass leans tearout: dry, punchy, mid-forward growls with fast LFO modulation rather than long evolving wobbles. Less reverberant than classic Skrillex-era dubstep.
  - Snare layering is a known fingerprint — multiple snare samples stacked with transient shaping for a wide, cracking hit (reflected in the production breakdown linked below).
  - Drops are dense and busy — frequent bass-pattern flips every 2-4 bars, very few stretches of repetition.
  - Vocal chops appear as melodic pre-drop hooks rather than dominating the drop itself.
- **Sample tracks from your library:**
  - Flip It - Tape B Remix
  - Rising Tides
  - Postman
  - Moment

## Read this yourself
Levity is a useful artist to study *after* Destin has the melodic-dubstep skeleton down, not before. The reason is in the bass design: tearout-flavored bass is the hardest target on Destin's leaderboard because it requires manual wavetable modulation, layered distortion chains, and granular re-synthesis tricks that don't ship in Ableton Live 12 Lite. Lite's Operator + Simpler will not get you there — Vital (free) or Serum (paid) is the realistic path, with Lite's stock effects (Saturator, EQ Eight, Compressor) handling the post-processing.

The Sounds of Collapse Splice pack is genuinely worth keeping in mind as a learning resource: it's Levity's *own* pack, and tearout-dubstep one-shots and bass loops give you the raw material to study his arrangement choices without having to recreate every patch from scratch. For first projects, Destin's path forward is "load the loops, study the structure, replicate the structure with simpler sounds, then upgrade the sound design as skills grow."

The "Lasership" EDM.com piece is more career-shape than production, but it gives useful context: Levity is a stage-show producer as much as a track producer, and his catalog is engineered to translate to a festival mainstage. That changes what "good" means in this corner of the genre — the priority is impact at scale, not subtle harmonic detail.
