# Tech house

## TL;DR
Four-on-the-floor club music at 124-128 BPM with rolling basslines, tight punchy drums, and sparse melodic hooks. The sweet spot between house's groove and techno's machine-driven precision. Built around a hypnotic kick-bass interplay and a heavy reliance on sidechain compression for that "pumping" feel.

## Cheat block
- **BPM:** 124-128. The festival- / club-mainstage standard.
- **Drums:**
  - **Kick on every beat** (four-on-the-floor): 1, 2, 3, 4.
  - **Open hi-hat on the "and"s** between beats — the classic house "tss-tss-tss".
  - **Tight clap or rim on beats 2 and 4** — the backbeat snap.
  - **1/16 percussion loop** for groove (shakers, congas, processed claps).
- **Bass:** bouncy, syncopated, **tied to the kick rhythm** — bass plays in the gaps between kicks (so the bass and the kick aren't fighting). Often a single sustained note with movement coming from filter automation or sidechain.
- **Melodic content:** sparse. A two-note synth stab, a vocal chop, a four-bar lead — that's it. Tech house leaves space.
- **Structure (16/32-bar club arrangement):**
  - Intro (32 bars): drums building, no bass yet.
  - **Drop A (32):** kick + bass + percussion + sparse hook.
  - Break (16): kick out, riser, vocal sample, build tension.
  - **Drop B (32):** all elements back; usually a slight twist on Drop A.
  - Outro (32): drum elements falling out one by one for the next DJ to mix into.
- **Sidechain compression:** ducking the bass volume on every kick hit. Creates the "pumping" feel that's the genre's signature.
- **Common keys:** A minor, D minor, F minor, G minor. Camelot 8A, 10A, 4A, 6A.
- **Anchor artists:**
  - **John Summit** (Destin's confirmed taste anchor)
  - **Fisher** (genre's mainstream breakout)
  - **Chris Lake**
  - **Solardo, Mau P, Cloonee** (the contemporary front line).

## Read this yourself
Tech house lives in the pocket between house's groove emphasis and techno's machine emphasis. The drums are quantized tight and feel almost robotic — that's the "tech" half — but the bassline is musical and human, with swing and small accents — that's the "house" half. Get those two things right and you're 80% of the way to a tech-house track.

The single defining technique is **sidechain compression**, where the bass channel's volume is automatically pulled down (~3-6 dB) every time the kick hits. The result is a rhythmic pumping — bass *pushes* up between kicks and ducks under each kick. Pull this up too aggressively and the track sounds amateurish; dial it just right and the kick and bass interlock without competing for the low end. In Ableton, the easiest way is to put a Compressor on the bass track, set its sidechain input to the kick track, and tweak threshold/ratio until the meter shows ~3-6 dB of gain reduction on each kick.

The other secret is **how much space tech house leaves**. Most beginner producers want to fill every measure with content; tech house pros do the opposite, deleting elements until only the load-bearing ones remain. A great tech house track can be six elements at a time: kick, bass, hat, clap, percussion loop, and a single hook. Less = more.
