# Bass house

## TL;DR
House at 125-130 BPM with louder, grittier, distorted basses, harder kicks, and frequent dubstep-leaning drops over a 4/4 groove. The bridge between house and dubstep — keeps the four-on-the-floor kick that powers the dancefloor but borrows dubstep's filthy bass-design vocabulary.

## Cheat block
- **BPM:** 125-130 (slightly faster than tech house).
- **Drums:**
  - **Four-on-the-floor kick** with hard, processed transient (often two layered kicks).
  - Backbeat clap on 2/4.
  - Open hat on the "and"s — same as tech house but punchier.
  - **Wider, more processed drums than tech house overall.**
- **Bass:** the centerpiece. Heavily distorted reese basses, growling sawtooth basses, dubstep-style bass design but **playing on every beat under the kick** rather than half-time. Saturation, multiband distortion, parallel compression all standard.
- **Drops:** alternate between four-on-the-floor bass stabs (like house) and dubstep-style bass switches (one bar of 4/4 → one bar of 1/16 wobbles). The bait-and-switch is part of the genre.
- **Structure:** mostly the 32-bar club arrangement of tech house, but the breakdowns lean more dramatic and the drops lean more aggressive.
- **Anchor artists:**
  - **Jigitz** (Destin's confirmed taste anchor)
  - **Habstrakt** (genre defining)
  - **JOYRYDE** (more bass-heavy end)
  - **Dr. Fresch, AC Slater, label: Confession**
- **Sound design defaults:** Serum/Vital + heavy distortion (FabFilter Saturn or Ableton's Saturator), parallel compression for thickness, multiband for taming low end, OTT (a free preset for Xfer's Multiband Compressor) for everything-louder.

## Read this yourself
Bass house took the rhythmic skeleton of house — four-on-the-floor kick, off-beat hats, club-mainstage tempo around 128 — and asked "what if the bass was as filthy and distorted as a dubstep bass?" The answer turned out to be a genre that crosses over to both house crowds and bass-music crowds: it's danceable enough for an Insomniac main stage, aggressive enough for a Bassrush room.

Production-wise, bass house is a more accessible target than dubstep because the rhythm is fixed (four-on-the-floor) and the structure is identical to house, so you don't have to make tempo and arrangement decisions while also wrestling with bass design. You write the drum loop and the chord progression first like you would for tech house, then spend your sound-design effort on **one** bass patch — the centerpiece — and a small handful of one-shot stabs.

The drop bait-and-switch is the genre's signature trick. Drop A plays straight four-on-the-floor bass stabs for 16 bars, then switches to dubstep-style 1/16 wobbles for a bar before dropping back into the four-on-the-floor pattern. The switch surprises the dancefloor without breaking the flow. Listen to a Jigitz track and you'll hear it three or four times per drop.
