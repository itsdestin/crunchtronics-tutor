# Dubstep

## TL;DR
Heavy 140 BPM bass music with a half-time kick-clap pulse and aggressive growling/wobble basses; covers riddim's repetitive triplet bass too. The cluster includes everything from classic Skrillex-era brostep to modern bass / heavy-dubstep heavyweights like Subtronics, plus the riddim sub-style with its repeating triplet bass patterns.

## Cheat block
- **BPM:** 140 (felt as 70 because of half-time drums).
- **Drums:** kick on beat 1, snare/clap on beat 3 (every other beat — the "half-time" feel). Hi-hat patterns are sparse, often 1/8 with rolls into drops.
- **Structure:** intro (16 bars) → first build (16) → first drop (32) → break (16) → second build (8-16) → second drop (32) → outro.
- **The drop:** bass takes over the lead role. There's no melodic instrument carrying the energy — it's all bass design.
- **Riddim sub-style:** repeating triplet bass pattern (often 3 hits per beat or 6 per beat) — punchy, dry, less reverberant than classic dubstep.
- **Anchor artists:**
  - **Subtronics** (riddim / heavy dubstep — Destin's confirmed taste anchor)
  - **Excision** (heavy dubstep, festival headliner)
  - **Virtual Riot** (technical sound design, dubstep / future bass crossover)
  - **Svdden Death** (riddim)
- **Sound design defaults:** Serum or Vital + heavy distortion (Saturator, OTT) + multiband compression + LFO/wavetable modulation for movement. Chains tend to be: oscillator → distortion → EQ carve → multiband → reverb send.
- **Common keys:** F minor, G minor, A minor — minor keys with low roots. Camelot codes 4A, 6A, 8A.

## Read this yourself
Dubstep started in Croydon, South London in the early 2000s as a slower, sparser cousin of UK garage. Skrillex took the half-time pulse and the bass-as-lead idea to the US in 2010 and the genre exploded — louder, faster sound design, more aggressive drops. The modern American "bass music" scene that dominates festivals like Lost Lands and Bass Canyon is the direct descendant of that moment.

Riddim split off around 2014 as a more minimal sub-style: instead of dense, ever-changing bass design like Skrillex or Excision, riddim producers built drops around one or two repeating triplet bass patterns and let the rhythm itself carry the weight. Subtronics is the genre's biggest contemporary name — his sets blend riddim's repetition with heavy-dubstep's sound-design density.

For producing: dubstep is not where to start. Sound design takes years to dial in, and Lite's Operator + Simpler aren't the obvious tools for it (Vital and Serum are). But it's worth understanding the structure — many of the same arrangement patterns (intro / build / drop / break / drop) recur in melodic dubstep and bass house, both of which are more accessible production targets.
