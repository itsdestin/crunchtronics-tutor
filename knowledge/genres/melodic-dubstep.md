# Melodic dubstep

## TL;DR
Anthemic 140 BPM bass music with emotive piano/lead intros, big chord progressions, and softer, more harmonic drops than classic dubstep. The "feels" cousin of heavy dubstep — same tempo and structural skeleton, but the drop is built around supersaw chord stabs and singing leads rather than aggressive growl basses, and the build is a piano/vocal moment.

## Cheat block
- **BPM:** 140, same half-time drum feel as dubstep.
- **Structure:**
  - **Intro (16 bars):** piano + vocal hook + ambient pad. Melodic, emotional, no drums or sparse drums.
  - **Build (16 bars):** add drums, supersaw layers, vocal layering. Filter risers, snare rolls.
  - **Drop (32 bars):** singing supersaw leads carrying the chord progression. Sub bass underneath. Less aggressive than classic dubstep.
  - **Break (16):** strip back to piano/pad/vocal — a second emotional moment.
  - **Second drop (32):** often the same as the first or a layered/intensified version.
- **Common chord progression:** **vi-IV-I-V** in minor (or its relative-major twin). Anthemic, hopeful — the melodic-dubstep signature.
- **Common keys:** C minor, D minor, F minor, G minor. Camelot 5A, 7A, 4A, 6A.
- **Anchor artists:**
  - **Wooli** (Destin's confirmed taste anchor)
  - **Seven Lions** (genre originator-ish; trance/melodic-dubstep crossover)
  - **Illenium** (mainstream face of the genre)
  - **Said the Sky**
- **Sound design defaults:** layered supersaws (Operator can do passable supersaw stacks; Serum/Vital are easier), sub bass below the leads, lots of reverb on pads. Vocals are often female, processed with formant shifting and pitch correction.

## Read this yourself
Melodic dubstep is what happens when trance's emotional vocabulary meets dubstep's structural skeleton. The build has the dramatic uplift trance fans recognize from a Tiësto record, but the drop hits at 140 BPM with half-time drums instead of trance's 132-138 four-on-the-floor. The bass design is much friendlier to beginners than heavy dubstep — instead of hand-modulating wavetables to get a unique growl, you stack three or four supersaws across an octave and let the chord progression do the heavy lifting.

The chord progression matters more here than in any other EDM genre. **vi-IV-I-V** (in C major: Am - F - C - G; in C minor's relative E♭ major: Cm - A♭ - E♭ - B♭) is everywhere — it's the "anthemic uplift" sound that festivals love. Once you can identify it, you'll start hearing it on every Wooli, Seven Lions, and Illenium release. (It's also the chord progression of Pachelbel's Canon, Don't Stop Believin', and roughly half of pop music since 2000. There's a reason.)

For first production projects, melodic dubstep is more accessible than heavy dubstep. The drop synths (supersaws, plucks) are stock-instrument-friendly, and the chord-progression-driven structure means you can write something that "feels right" before you've mastered sound design.
