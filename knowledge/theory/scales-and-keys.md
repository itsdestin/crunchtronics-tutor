# Scales and keys

## TL;DR
Major and minor scales, key signatures, and how a track's key shapes its mood. A scale is a recipe of intervals; a key is that scale anchored to a starting note. Most EDM lives in minor keys because they read as moodier and more cinematic.

## Cheat block
- **Major scale:** seven notes, pattern **W-W-H-W-W-W-H** (W = whole step, H = half step). C major = C D E F G A B C — all white keys.
- **Natural minor scale:** **W-H-W-W-H-W-W**. A minor = A B C D E F G A — also all white keys, but starting on A.
- **Key:** scale + root note. "F minor" = the natural minor scale starting on F.
- **12 root notes × 2 modes = 24 keys.** (More if you count the other modes — ignore those for now.)
- **Mood (very rough):** major = bright, hopeful, anthemic; minor = dark, moody, nostalgic. Most EDM is minor.
- **Modes** (Dorian, Phrygian, etc.) are variations of the same notes starting on a different scale degree. **Skip them on first pass.**
- **MPK Scales Mode:** locks the keys to a chosen scale so you can't play wrong notes. Pick root + scale type and the rest of the keyboard rearranges to fit.

## Read this yourself
The single most useful sentence in early music theory: "in the key of A minor" means "this track is built from the seven notes A B C D E F G, and A is the home note that everything resolves to." That's the whole concept. The key gives you a palette of seven notes that sound good together; everything else is just choices about *which* of those seven you reach for and *when*.

The MPK Mini 4's Scales Mode is genuinely a beginner superpower. Set it to A minor and your keyboard physically can't play a wrong note — every key you press belongs to that key. If you're writing a melody on top of a track you already love, look up that track's key (your `taste/tracks.csv` will eventually have it under `key_camelot`), set Scales Mode to match, and noodle until something clicks. The training wheels come off later.
