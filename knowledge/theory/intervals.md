# Intervals

## TL;DR
Distances between two notes, named in semitones and scale steps. An interval is just "how far apart" two notes are — measured both as a raw count of piano keys (semitones) and as a stepped name (third, fifth, etc.) that depends on the scale you're in.

## Cheat block
- **Semitone (half step):** distance from one piano key to the next, white or black.
- **Whole step:** two semitones.
- **Octave:** twelve semitones — the next time the same note name comes back around.
- **Standard interval names** (within an octave):
  - **m2** (minor 2nd) = 1 semitone
  - **M2** (major 2nd) = 2 semitones (whole step)
  - **m3** (minor 3rd) = 3 semitones
  - **M3** (major 3rd) = 4 semitones
  - **P4** (perfect 4th) = 5 semitones
  - **TT** (tritone) = 6 semitones (the unstable one)
  - **P5** (perfect 5th) = 7 semitones
  - **m6, M6, m7, M7, P8** continuing the pattern
- **Consonant** (sounds resolved): P4, P5, P8, M3, m3, M6, m6.
- **Dissonant** (sounds tense): m2, M2, M7, m7, TT.

## Read this yourself
The piano keyboard is a brilliant visual cheat sheet for intervals. Look at the two-and-three black-key clusters — they repeat every octave, which is exactly twelve white-and-black keys. Counting "one, two, three" up from any starting key gives you the semitone count to that destination key. After a while, the common intervals start to feel like physical distances: a fifth is a comfortable hand spread, an octave is a full reach.

Why bother memorizing the names? Because chords are stacks of intervals (a major chord is root + M3 + P5) and melodies are sequences of intervals. Once you can hear the difference between a "third leap" and a "fifth leap", you stop placing notes blindly and start writing lines that go where you want them to.
