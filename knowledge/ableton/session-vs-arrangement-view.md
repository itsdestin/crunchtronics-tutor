# Session vs Arrangement view

## TL;DR
Session view is a clip-launching grid; Arrangement view is a linear timeline; you can move between them. **Session** is for jamming and trying things — clips arranged in a grid you trigger by hand. **Arrangement** is for laying out a finished song — clips placed on a timeline you scroll through. Most beginners sketch in Session and commit to Arrangement.

## Cheat block
- **Session view:** vertical grid. Columns = tracks, rows = scenes. Click a clip to launch it; click a scene row to launch every clip in that row.
- **Arrangement view:** horizontal timeline. Tracks stack vertically, time runs left-to-right. The "DAW" most other software starts in.
- **Toggle:** **Tab** key flips between the two views.
- **Lite floor:** **8 audio + 8 MIDI tracks**, period. Not enough to be sloppy — pick what each track is for early.
- **Recording from Session into Arrangement:** the round circle in the Arrangement transport. Hit it, then play your Session clips — Ableton prints them as a real timeline.
- **What lives where:** clip data is shared (a clip in Session is the same MIDI/audio as the same clip in Arrangement), but launching state is per-view.

## Read this yourself
Session view is what makes Ableton famous — it's the "live performance" surface, where every clip is a button you hit. But for a beginner producing a tune, Session is also the world's best sketchpad. Drop in a four-bar drum loop on track 1, a bass on track 2, a chord stab on track 3, and start launching combinations. You'll find the four bars that feel right far faster than if you were drawing them onto a fixed timeline.

When the loop locks in, hit the Arrangement record (red circle in the top transport), play through your sections in order — intro / drop / break / drop — and Ableton prints the whole thing out as a real arrangement. That's your starting structure. From there, switch to Arrangement view (Tab) and edit it like a normal DAW timeline.

The Lite floor — 8 audio + 8 MIDI — sounds tight, but it's enough for a finished tech-house track if you stay disciplined. Sub bass, kick, snare/clap, hat, percussion loop, lead, pad, vocal chop — that's eight, and you've got a song.
